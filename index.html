Depression

Depression is a state of low mood and aversion to activity that can dictate a personâ€™s behavior, thoughts, feelings and sense of well-being. People who are depressed can feel anxious, empty, sad, lost, hopeless, helpless, worthless, guilty, restless, ashamed, or irritable. They can lose interest in things that they used to do for fun, lose their appetite and or overeat, have issues with concentration, recalling details or making decisions. People who are depressed may contemplate, attempt or commit suicide. Insomnia, lethargy, fatigue, pains, aches, gastrointestinal issues or low energy levels may also be present. Depression is part of some psychiatric syndromes such as major depressive order, but it may also be a normal reaction to life events like grief, a side effect of some drugs and medical treatments or a symptom of some bodily ailments.

Anxiety

Anxiety is an emotion characterized by an unpleasant state of inner turmoil, at times coupled with a nervous behavior, like pacing, somatic complaints (the physical symptoms of anxiety) and rumination (compulsively focused attention on ones distress). It is a subjectively unpleasant feeling of dread over anticipated events, like a feeling of imminent death. Anxiety is a feeling of fear, worry and uneasiness typically generalized and unfocused as an overreaction to a situation that is subjectively menacing. It is usually accompanied by muscular tension, restlessness, fatigue, and trouble concentrating.

Therapy

Therapy is a common form of treatment for depression and anxiety. In many instances, therapy can be individualized so that it can work to reduce both disorders. Many types of psychotherapy are useful. Cognitive-behavior therapy (CBT) works to replace unproductive and negative thought patterns with more useful, realistic ones. Treatment involves facing your fears as part of recovery. Interpersonal therapy and problem-solving therapy can also be effective.

Medication

Medications can also be helpful. Depression and anxiety symptoms often occur together. Research shows that both respond to treatment with SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitor) and SNRI (Serotonin Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitor) medications. Other medications can be used if SSRI or SNRI does not provide enough improvement. If you have severe symptoms or functional limitations, medication treatment may be combined with psychotherapy.

Self Harm

Self harm is form of expressing and dealing with emotional pain and deep distress. Although it can sound counterintuitive, hurting yourself makes you feel better. In fact, you may feel like you donâ€™t have a choice. Injuring yourself is your only way to cope with feelings like rage, emptiness, guilt, sadness, and self-loathing. The problem is that the relief that self harm provides does not last very long. It doesn't fix the issue causing the behavior, and can create other problems. If you are someone who injures themselves, you likely try and keep it a secret. You might feel ashamed or could think no one will understand you. But hiding what you feel and who you are, is a heavy burden to carry. In the long run, secrecy and guilt affects your relationships with family, friends, and how you feel about yourself. It can make you feel trapped, worthless and lonely.

Community

A great way to combat feelings of depression and anxiety is to get out into the community. Whether it is taking workout classes at a gym, the tranquility of a yoga class, making connections in a support group with people who are dealing with similar issues, getting out of your own head and outside yourself helps.
